Florida’s Affordable Housing Crisis: A Data-Driven View

1. Overview & Problem Statement

The United States is facing a deepening affordable housing crisis, with a national shortage of over 7 million affordable homes impacting more than
10.8 million extremely low-income families. In every state, even working full time at minimum wage is not enough to afford a modest two-bedroom apartment.
Florida, in particular, exemplifies this crisis. With rapidly rising housing costs, significant environmental vulnerabilities,
and financial barriers to homeownership, the state presents a complex challenge.


Key consequences include:

  • 70% of low-income familiesstrong> are severely cost-burdened, spending over half their income on rent.
  • A growing number of residents are displaced or forced into “super commutes.”
  • Climate vulnerabilities, particularly flooding, disproportionately impact Black, Hispanic, and low-income communities.

This case study focuses on an analytical project conducted as part of the DataKind Challenge,
using open-source data and demographic insights to examine housing challenges in Florida for the year 2020.


2. Objective

The goal of this analysis was to:
  • Identify systemic barriers to housing affordability and accessibility in Florida.
  • Examine the intersection of environmental risk, financial access, and socioeconomic inequality.
  • Provide insights that can inform policy, investment, and community-level interventions.

3. Methodology & Data Sources

To enable a holistic and multidimensional analysis, the study used U.S. Census data, mapping files,
and datasets provided by DataKind, organized into five main categories:


Dataset Focus
Housing Availability, pricing, quality across regions
Banking Mortgage rates, lending access, financial resources
Environment Flood risk, pollution, proximity to services
Economic Profile Income levels, employment rates, economic conditions
Integrated Combined view for geographic and systemic analysis

4. Key Findings & Insights


4.1 Demographic Disparities

  • As of 2020, Florida’s population was 36.8% White, 28.3% Black, 5.2% Asian,
    with 26% identifying as multi-racial or from other groups     including Hispanic/Latino populations.
  • By 2022, non-Hispanic White population rose to 52.3%, but Hispanic/Latino growth led with a >4.5 percentage point increase, emphasizing the state's increasing diversity.
  • Many minorities are overrepresented in high-risk flood zones, confirming environmental justice concerns (Mittan, 2020; Backenssen & Ma, 2020).

4.2 Housing Affordability

  • Rents increased over 30% between 2020 and 2022.
  • Over one-third of low-income residents now spend more than 30% of income on rent, squeezing budgets for essentials.
  • Homeownership rates (~64-65%) lag slightly behind the national average, with urban renters disproportionately impacted by rising costs.

4.3 Financial Constraints

  • Florida’s Purchase Applications Payment Index (PAPI) reached 228strong>, indicating one of the highest cost burdens nationally.
  • Median mortgage payments in 2024 were $2,600/monthstrong>, driven by prices exceeding $415,000strong> and rates above 7%strong>.
  • High denial rates and tightened lending standards disproportionately impact
    low-income and minority applicantsstrong>, especially in urban counties.

4.4 Environmental Risk & Exposure


  • Pollution sources include plastics, phosphate mining, fertilizersstrong>, and aging infrastructurestrong>.
  • Areas with the highest pollution levelsstrong> (counties 20–27) also tend to have lower incomestrong> and higher minority populationsstrong>.
  • Common environmental burdens include:
    • Wastewater contamination
    • Air toxics
    • Diesel pollution
    • Lead exposure in older housing stock

4.5 Intersections and Systemic Risk


  • Combined analysis reveals that financial, environmental, and economic challenges are not isolatedstrong>,
    but compound for certain populations.
  • Flood-prone zones overlap with affordable housing clusters, forcing vulnerable residents into high-riskstrong> living situations.
  • Environmental justice is a key lens through which housing policy must be evaluated

6. Policy & Design Considerations


The data surfaced a number of opportunities that could inform policy and program design:

  • Targeted investment in affordable housing could help increase access in areas with lower environmental risk.
  • Promoting equitable lending practices, including anti-discrimination enforcement and incentives for fair credit access, may improve financial inclusion.
  • Incorporating climate resilience into housing development—particularly in coastal or flood-prone areas—could strengthen long-term stability.
  • Addressing environmental burdens in lower-income communities may contribute to improved health outcomes and livability.
  • Leveraging integrated datasetsstrong> could support more responsive, data-driven decision-making across housing and environmental planning.

7. Conclusion


Florida’s affordable housing crisis is complex and far-reaching. It affects not just where people live, but how they live — touching everything from income and race to flood risk and health. By bringing together data on housing, finances, the environment, and community demographics, this project paints a clearer picture of who is most affected and why. This work shows that data isn’t just numbers — it’s a tool for understanding real lives. When we connect the dots, we can see where systems are failing and where action is most needed. With the right insights, we can move toward solutions that are not only practical, but fair — helping create safer, more affordable homes for all Floridians.
